This combination of improvements in hardware and software and decreasing costs has led to the explosion of the use of computers in public health research during the last 15 years. In the early 1960s, IBM mainframes were appearing in large university research centres. However, researchers needed to estimate in advance the computer space, time, and power needed to complete the computational aspects of their work. The lack of predictability of these parameters, coupled with cost constraints and the restrictions of the available statistical programs, made use of computers in research difficult. In spite of these barriers, the sheer amount of information the computer could handle at such tremendous speed made those researchers with the personnel and financial resources incorporate them in their work. In the last decade, with the proliferation of mincomputers, the use of computers in public health research and practice has become universal. The user of the computer is no longer a trained computer scientist but rather a physician, laboratory scientist, secretary, librarian, or student.
